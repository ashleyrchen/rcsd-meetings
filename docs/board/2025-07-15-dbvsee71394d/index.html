Reference(s) Curriculum development and approval is an ongoing activity overseen by the Academic Senate, and its subordinate Curriculum Committee, and the Office of Instruction as per District Policy 4020. It is emphasized in many sections of Standard II of the Accreditation Standards of the Accrediting Commission for Community and Junior Colleges. Likewise, it is regulated through Title 5 and WVMCCD district Policy 4020. Background/Alternatives As per district policy, the curriculum changes to programs and courses described herein have been considered and approved by the Curriculum Committee and the Articulation Officer. Degrees developed in compliance with State-approved Transfer Model Curricula (TMC) pursuant to SB-1440 (Padilla, 2010) have already been approved by the District Chancellor acting as designee for the Board of Trustees and are being presented here as information items. Coordination Offerings have been coordinated between Mission College and West Valley College through a process of consultation. Follow-up/Outcome Following Board approval of the curriculum changes, updates to catalogs and class schedules will be made, and campus marketing communications will reflect the changes, as appropriate. Courses which are archived will be removed from the College catalog; courses which are deleted will be removed both from the College catalog and from the state Curriculum Inventory. §

Prepared By: Jeffrey Pallin Reviewed By: Lorrie Ranck and Seher Awan Approved By: Bradley Davis Funding Source/Fiscal Impact This contract will be funded by Mission College’s California Apprenticeship Initiative (CAI) 2024 – 2027 Implementation Grant. The original grant award of $947,565 began on August 1, 2024, and extends through December 31, 2027. As of April 30, 2025, the balance in the Fund is $793,185.90. Reference(s) Apprenticeship aligns with Goal 1, Objective 3 of the Mission College Approved Strategic Education Master Plan. “Cultivate experiential and work-based learning programs such as internships and apprenticeships.” Development of nontraditional, innovative apprenticeship programs aligns with Vision 2030 from the State Chancellor’s Office: Strategic Direction 2, Equitable Workforce and Economic Development, and helps meet the Governor’s goal of 500,000 apprentices in California by 2029. Background/Alternatives The college previously used its 2023-2024 $120,000 CAI Planning Grant to register the Mechatronics Certificate Program with the U.S. Department of Labor and the California Division of Apprenticeship Standards. T his Apprenticeship Program for Hardware Technician now partners with Applied Materials of Santa Clara as the original employer sponsor. Currently, there are 3 employer-organizations with incumbent employees enrolled as students in the college’s Mechatronics Certificate of Achievement program for Hardware Technician. 66Training is in discussions with 5 new employer-organizations who are visiting the college’s Mechatronics lab and classroom. These employers will either hire college students as apprentices or enroll current employees as students. Coordination The dean of workforce innovation oversees apprenticeship, internship, work experience, on-the-job training, and career/workforce industry partners, and manages the college’s two CTE Grants – Strong Workforce and Perkins. The Workforce Innovation team is responsible for collecting all data necessary for timely and accurate reporting to the state, as the college is required to submit ongoing, regular reporting of progress in the successful application for all grant-supported programs to gauge the success of the effort and its impact on students and the region Follow-up/Outcome Upon Board approval, this contract will go into effect July 16, 2025, through June 30, 2026. §

ADOPT RESOLUTION NO. 25071502 AUTHORIZING LEGISLATIVE ACTION TO EXCLUDE FEDERALLY DEFUNDED DEIA PROGRAM EXPENDITURES FROM THE 50 PERCENT LAW CALCULATION (2025-2026) — Recommendation: The Board of Trustees adopt Resolution No. 25071502, authorizing the chancellor to pursue legislation permitting the temporary exclusion of federally defunded DEIA program expenditures from the 50 Percent Law calculation for 2025-2026.

Prepared By: Cade Story-Yetto Reviewed By: Cade Story-Yetto Approved By: Bradley Davis Funding Source/Fiscal Impact The district is prepared to maintain funding for federal student support programs (including TRIO, Title V-HSI, and AANAPISI) by utilizing local funds in the event of a federal funding reduction. Should these federal DEIA dollars be eliminated, the district plans to absorb costs locally through existing unrestricted general funds. However, under current law, using these local funds for student support services would increase the district’s Current Expense of Education (CEE) denominator, placing the district at risk of noncompliance with the 50 Percent Law. This resolution seeks a statutory carve-out to avoid forcing a choice between legal compliance and continued support for vulnerable student populations. Reference(s) The “Fifty Percent Law” (50% Law), as defined in Education Code Section 84362 and California Code of Regulations Section 59200 et seq., requires each community college district to spend at least half of its current expense of education each fiscal year on salaries and benefits of classroom instructors. State Auditor’s Report 2023-126, California Community Colleges: Oversight of the 50 Percent Law Is Ineffective, and the Law Could Be Amended to Better Support Students (April 2025). Community College League of California Report: Modernizing California Community College Funding: Reforming the Fifty Percent Law to Support Student Success (March 2025). Student Senate for California Community Colleges, Resolution S25.09.03 Revision of the 50% Law to Support Student Services (March 2025). Background/Alternatives California’s 50 Percent Law, enacted in 1961, requires districts to allocate at least half of their unrestricted educational expenditures to classroom instructor salaries. While this law was initially intended to ensure strong instructional investment, it now places spending on essential student success infrastructure and support services—including counselors, librarians, basic needs coordinators, and technology—on the “non-instructional” side of the formula. As a result, districts investing local funds to sustain these functions risk falling out of compliance with the law, even when such investments are indispensable to student retention, completion, and equity. Recent statewide reports, including those from the State Auditor, the Community College League of California, and the Student Senate for California Community Colleges, have affirmed that the current formula penalizes districts for using local funds to support student success, calling for a modernization of the law. With significant federal funding at risk for programs such as TRIO, Title V-HSI, AANAPISI, and others, districts across California may be compelled to either cut critical services or jeopardize compliance with the 50 Percent Law. This resolution authorizes the chancellor to work with state legislators and regional partners to pursue legislation that: A) Secures a five-year exclusion of expenditures for federal student support programs (such as TRIO, Title V-HSI, and AANAPISI) from the 50 Percent Law calculation when local funds are used to backfill federal cuts; B) Preserves transparency and public reporting of these expenditures; and C) Sunsets the carve-out upon restoration of federal funding or at the end of the defined period, whichever comes first. Coordination This resolution was reviewed by the Legislative Policy & Oversight Committee at its June 11, 2025, meeting and was unanimously recommended for Board adoption. Follow-up/Outcome If adopted, the resolution will be posted on each campus and made available on the district’s website. The approved resolution will also be provided to the State Chancellor’s Office and appropriate legislative partners. The Board will receive updates on progress toward statutory change and further action as needed. §
