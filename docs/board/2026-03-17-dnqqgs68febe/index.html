Acceptance of a $2,270,000 gift to the West Valley-Mission Community College District (WVMCCD) Foundation from Howard Gore and Kimberly Hawkins-Gore - Recommendation: The Board of Trustees authorize the WVMCCD Foundation and the district administration to accept a new $2,270,000 pledge from Howard Gore and Kimberly Hawkins-Gore.

Prepared By: Justin Tran Reviewed By: Melissa Johns Approved By: Bradley Davis Funding Source/Fiscal Impact The WVMCCD Foundation received a multi-year $2,270,000 pledge from Howard Gore and Kimberly Hawkins-Gore to continue their support of scholarships, student grants, and experiential learning programs at West Valley College and Mission College. Added to their December 2025 donation of $230,000, this pledge signifies a total gift commitment of $2.5 million. In recognition of their generosity, the district proposes the following honorary facility naming at West Valley College: The Howard and Kimberly Gore Student Services Center. A named facility in their honor would stand as a visible and enduring expression of their belief in our students and their confidence in our district’s ability to transform lives and communities. The donors will make annual contributions to support students in unpaid internships, scholarships, grants for nursing students, and business incubator grants for students who are starting or growing their own business. The donors will also establish an endowment to create an ongoing source of funds for initiatives that give students meaningful, real-world work experience as part of their academic journey. The donors’ first installment in the amount of $230,000 was received by the WVMCCD Foundation in December 2025. The donors intend to fulfill the remaining $2,270,000 donation according to the following pledge schedule: $1 million over five years ($215,000 annually 2026-29 and $140,000 in 2030) will provide direct program support for unpaid internships, business incubator grants, and scholarships. $900,000 in Spring 2026 will establish the principal of a new endowment at West Valley College to support student internships and work experience programming. $300,000 in additional endowment contributions ($100,000 annually, 2026–2028) will increase the endowment principal. $70,000 in 2029 will further augment the endowment principal. Reference(s) Board Policy 3820 Gifts: Governs acceptance of donations exceeding $10,000, which require prior Board approval. Background/Alternatives Howard Gore and Kimberly Hawkins-Gore are strong supporters of both West Valley College and Mission College. Their continued contributions reflect a strong commitment to student success, workforce preparation, and access to higher education. Coordination The gift was coordinated by associate vice chancellor of advancement Melissa Johns, president Jennifer Taylor-Mendoza, and vice president of strategy and equity Andrea Vizenor. Follow-up/Outcome The Office of Advancement and the WVMCCD Foundation will be responsible for the acceptance of the gift, accurate accounting of funds, endowment investment, and disbursement of scholarships and student grants. §

ADOPT RESOLUTION NO. 26031702 COMMITMENT TO ADDRESSING HOUSING INSECURITY AMONG STUDENTS THROUGH AN INTERSEGMENTAL HOUSING PARTNERSHIP WITH SAN JOSÉ STATE UNIVERSITY - Recommendation: The Board of Trustees adopt Resolution No. 26031702 COMMITMENT TO ADDRESSING HOUSING INSECURITY AMONG STUDENTS THROUGH AN INTERSEGMENTAL HOUSING PARTNERSHIP WITH SAN JOSÉ STATE UNIVERSITY.

Prepared By: Cade Story-Yetto Reviewed By: Ngoc Chim Approved By: Bradley Davis Funding Source/Fiscal Impact The district's community support (Basic Aid) funds will be allocated to support the costs of the intersegmental housing partnership pilot with San José State University, in alignment with the board's commitment to removing housing insecurity as a barrier to student success and reducing the cost of education for students who need it most. The district will fully subsidize all costs associated with housing participating students, including room, meal plan, applicable SJSU fees, and supplemental service costs. The estimated amount set aside to support this initiative is $500,000, which has been incorporated into the district's budget planning. The chancellor has committed sufficient unrestricted general fund dollars to sustain and scale this partnership through the pilot period and beyond. Reference(s) Goal 2 of the 2025-26 Annual Board of Trustees Goals prioritizes the management of district assets to meet student needs and explicitly highlights the exploration of housing partnerships as a key measurement of success. Assembly Bill 648 (2021), codified in Education Code Section 17021.1, authorizes California community college districts to construct and maintain student housing, establishing the legislature's recognition of community colleges' independent role in addressing housing insecurity among their student populations. Board Policy 6910 (Housing) delegates to the chancellor the authority to enter into housing agreements on behalf of the district. Background/Alternatives Over the past three years, the district has leveraged local funds to systematically remove financial barriers for students, including the elimination of tuition, parking, healthcare, textbook, childcare, and other miscellaneous fees, as well as the establishment of a universal meal program. Housing insecurity remains the most acute barrier still facing the district's students. Data indicate that approximately 43% of the district’s students experience housing insecurity, including students residing in shelters, vehicles, or unstable temporary arrangements. Research consistently demonstrates that housing insecurity is among the strongest predictors of attrition in higher education, with housing insecure students significantly less likely to persist term-to-term and substantially more likely to reduce course loads, withdraw, or stop out entirely. When a student's most basic need for stable shelter is unmet, it functionally undermines every other investment the district makes in their success. The district has identified an intersegmental housing partnership with San José State University as the most operationally viable and immediately impactful approach to addressing this need. SJSU maintains existing dormitory infrastructure with 24-hour residential life operations and has vacancies during periods of reduced occupancy. The pilot envisions housing district students with documented, persistent housing insecurity in existing SJSU dormitory vacancies. An initial cohort of up to 20 students will be housed beginning April 1, 2026, with continuity through the end of the SJSU summer term on July 25. Beginning May 23, the district anticipates expanding to approximately 75 total students for the summer term. This resolution authorizes the chancellor to execute a Memorandum of Understanding with San José State University and to take all actions necessary to operationalize the housing partnership pilot for the Spring-Summer 2026 period, with the expectation that the partnership will continue into subsequent terms as capacity and institutional alignment allow. Coordination The chancellor developed this resolution and the underlying partnership framework in coordination with the executive management team and SJSU's Department of Housing. District legal counsel has been consulted to ensure compliance on matters related to Title IX, the Clery Act, FERPA, cross-institutional conduct jurisdiction, among others. Follow-up/Outcome Upon adoption, the chancellor will oversee the execution of a Memorandum of Understanding with San José State University and the launch of the housing partnership pilot. The district will produce a formal impact report at the conclusion of the pilot period, drawing on academic outcome data, student surveys, and qualitative case management observations. During the summer, the district will reassess its fiscal position and present the Board of Trustees with a comprehensive report on the partnership's effectiveness, along with any recommended expansion, modifications, or additional investment. §
