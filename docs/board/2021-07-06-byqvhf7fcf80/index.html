PROPOSITION 30 EDUCATION PROTECTION ACCOUNT (EPA) SPENDING PLAN - Recommendation: The Board of Trustees approve the spending plan for the Education Protection Account (EPA) for fiscal year 2021-22.

Prepared By: Ngoc Chim Reviewed By: Bradley Davis Approved By: Bradley Davis Funding Source/Fiscal Impact The State continues to provide Proposition 30 revenues in FY 2021-22 to support local schools and community colleges. The funding amount is $100 per FTES. The District anticipates receiving approximately $1,170,000 from the State and intends to use the money for instructional salaries and benefits. A separate sub-fund has been established in the general ledger to track the revenue and expenditures for the EPA funding. After the close of the fiscal year, the District will publish the actual revenue received and the actual expenditures on the District’s website. Reference(s) On November 6, 2012, voters approved Proposition 30, The Schools and Local Public Safety Protection Act. This proposition temporarily raises the sales and use tax by 0.25 cents for four years and raises the income tax rate for high income earners ($250,000 for individuals and $500,000 for couples) for seven years to provide continuing funding for local school districts and community colleges. The sales and use tax expired on January 1, 2017. The income tax is expected to expire on December 31, 2018. The revenues generated from Proposition 30 are deposited into a newly created state account called the Educational Protection Account (EPA). On November 8, 2016, voters approved Proposition 55, The California Children’s Education and Health Care Protection Act of 2016, which is an extension of Proposition 30 extending the personal income tax increase through 2030-31. The accounting and reporting requirements for Proposition 55 are exactly the same as the requirements for Proposition 30. The language of Proposition 30 requires that each local educational agency”…shall have sole authority to determine how the moneys received from the EPA are spent…provided, however, that the appropriate governing board or body shall make these spending determinations in open session of a public meeting of the governing board or body and shall not use any of the funds from the EPA for salaries or benefits of administrators or any other administrative costs.” Each local educational agency will be required to annually publish on its website an accounting of how much money was received from the EPA and how that money was spent. Revenues from EPA funds are unrestricted and should be accounted for separately. Background/Alternatives The District is required to comply with the language in Proposition 30. The District’s auditor’s will be reviewing for compliance at year-end. Coordination District Staff will work with college staff to record the revenue and expense to ensure the District is in compliance with Proposition 30 and the State Budget and Accounting Manual (BAM), which has the authority of regulation in accordance with Title 5 Section 59011 of the California Code of Regulations. Follow-up/Outcome Upon Board approval, the spending plan will be implemented. §

8.2 · Action · Business and Finance

CONSULTING SERVICES TO ANALYZE MARKET DEMAND AND FINANCIAL VIABILITY FOR THE PURPOSES OF DETERMINING THE FEASIBILITY OF POTENTIAL STUDENT, FACULTY, OR STAFF HOUSING ON WEST VALLEY AND MISSION COLLEGE CAMPUSES. Recommendation:The Board of Trustees authorize the administration to engage in a contract for consulting services with Scion Advisory Services (Scion) for market demand, financial analysis, and feasibility of potential housing for students, faculty or staff on West Valley and Mission College campuses for an amount not to exceed $75,000, effective July 7, 2021.

Prepared By: Manny Cappello Reviewed By: Bradley Davis Approved By: Bradley Davis Funding Source/Fiscal Impact This contract will be funded from the general fund and community support fund. Reference(s) Government Code Section 53060 allows the district to contract for special services and advice. Scion proposes to spend significant time listening to students, faculty, staff, and other stakeholders, and then gathering and synthesizing relevant data for both Mission College and West Valley College. The data will be generated from two sources: the colleges (recruitment and retention strategies, colleges&rsquo; goals, etc.) and by Scion through market and peer comparisons, electronic surveys, interviews and focus groups. Scion will develop a thorough understanding of the preferences and expectations for both the colleges and potential student residents, particularly in relation to demand, unit-types, amenities, services, cost, and in-kind supports. Evaluation of demand is critical to a successful housing project such as this. With numerous success stories of predicting accurate, reliable demand projections, Scion is a nationally recognized leader in conducting such analyses. Through their years of experience, they use relevant market data and precise analytics so the district can make the most informed decisions. Scion is the market leader in California for providing consulting services to community colleges in evaluating the potential for housing complexes as part of their business model. Community colleges currently utilizing Scion for their services include Santa Rosa Junior College, Napa Valley College, San Mateo Community College District, Ventura City College, Orange Coast College, College of the Redwoods, College of the Siskiyous, Chabot-Las Positas Community College District, Antelope Valley Community College District, Riverside Community College District, and Palomar College. Background/Alternatives The Silicon Valley housing market is extremely difficult for relocation of individuals and families. The barriers to entering the local housing market are daunting, having varied impacts on faculty and staff with differing income levels and living conditions. The housing crisis has had an adverse impact on recruitment and retention of faculty and staff. The district endeavors to evaluate the plausibility of housing services for faculty and staff as part of its overall commitment to improve employee retention, attract faculty and staff candidates in national searches, and build socioeconomic equity for its employees. The Silicon Valley housing market is also extremely difficult for students attending West Valley and Mission Colleges. Lack of affordable housing for students is driving rising levels of housing insecurity and homelessness among college students. Not too long ago in 2019, nearly one in five community college students in California experienced homelessness. In order to learn and thrive, students need to have their basic needs met. Scion has committed to provide us with the planning and feasibility services, operations assessment and development consulting we need to successfully evaluate the future potential of housing projects at each of our campus sites. The breadth of Scion&rsquo;s consultation experience in campus housing provides a high level of financial and real estate sophistication coupled with the perspectives of educators and operators. Scion has assisted many community college districts like WVMCCD find the path to success using a wide array of tools within a full suite of services. Coordination This contract has been reviewed by the director of general services, the associate vice chancellor for governmental affairs and public communications, and the chancellor. Follow-up/Outcome Upon Board approval, a consulting services contract will be issued to Scion for $75,000. Upon completion of Scion&rsquo;s analysis, a report will be generated and the results will be shared broadly with the campus and district communities, including participatory governance groups, and the Board of Trustees. A discussion of the report with Scion experts and any recommended next steps will be agendized at a regular board meeting. §

WEST VALLEY-MISSION COMMUNITY COLLEGES DISTRICT RESOLUTION 21070602 SUPPORTING ASSEMBLY BILL 306, SCHOOL DISTRICTS AND COMMUNITY COLLEGE DISTRICTS EMPLOYEE HOUSING ACT OF 2021, INTRODUCED BY ASSEMBLY MEMBER O’DONNELL. Recommendation:The Board of Trustees adopt Resolution No. 21070602 supporting Assembly Bill 306 expediting the development of community college district employee housing projects important for recruitment and retention of faculty and staff.

Prepared By: Manny Cappello Reviewed By: Bradley Davis Approved By: Bradley Davis Funding Source/Fiscal Impact There is no fiscal impact. Reference(s) Board Policies 2510 (Participation in Local Decision-Making), 3250 (Institutional Planning), 3280 (Grants), 3410 (Nondiscrimination), 3500 (Campus Safety), 3820 (Gifts), 6100 (Delegation of Authority), 6150 (Designation of Authorized Signatures), 6200 (Budget Preparation), 6240 (Allocation of Community Support Funds), 6250 (Budget Management), 6300 (Fiscal Management), 6340 (Contracts), 6400 (Audits), 6500 (Property Management), 6600 (Capital Construction), 6620 (Naming of Facilities & Other Naming Opportunities); and Education Code Sections 66030, 66250 et seq., 87100 et seq., and 87100 et seq.; Title 5 sections 53000 et seq., and 59300 et seq.; and Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. Background/Alternatives One of the barriers to successful recruitment and retention of employees at West Valley and Mission Colleges is the ever-worsening housing crisis in the San Francisco Bay Area. The development of faculty & staff housing provides an opportunity to create housing stability for employees, improve employee retention, and attract faculty and staff candidates in national searches. Assembly Bill 306 exempts a school district employee housing project from needing Department of State Architect approval before proceeding with construction.
